My research studies how monetary and fiscal policy interact with household heterogeneity to shape inflation, inequality, and the transmission of macroeconomic shocks.

Job Market Paper

Unfunded Fiscal Policy with Heterogeneity in Household Portfolios
Slides (20 minutes)

I build a heterogeneous-agent New Keynesian model with incomplete markets, endogenous portfolio choice, and long-term nominal debt to study unfunded fiscal expansions not fully backed by higher future primary surpluses. Unfunded shocks work through price-level revaluation and portfolio rebalancing: inflation reduces the real value of outstanding nominal liabilities, redistributes wealth from nominal creditors to net debtors, and raises spending by high-MPC households. In the calibrated model, unfunded government purchases and transfers generate similar impulse responses and larger multipliers than funded policies, while compressing consumption inequality.

Working Papers

Taylor Rules and Fiscal Attention to Inflation

I analyze equilibrium determinacy in a New Keynesian model where the primary surplus rule responds to both inflation and government debt and directly shifts the intertemporal IS curve. When fiscal policy targets inflation but not debt, I derive an analytical condition linking the monetary and fiscal inflation responses and show that determinacy can hold even with a Taylor coefficient below one if fiscal policy reacts strongly enough to inflation. When fiscal policy also stabilizes debt, numerical results show that part of the fiscal inflation response is absorbed by debt stabilization, so monetary policy must react more than one-for-one to inflation, with the required Taylor coefficient increasing in the degree of fiscal attention to inflation.

Identifying the Effects of Funded vs. Unfunded Fiscal Shocks (with Nathan S. Balke and Carlos Zarazaga)

We estimate a structural VAR for U.S. annual data using sign and zero restrictions on the present value of primary surpluses and the market value of government debt to distinguish funded deficit shocks—those offset by future surpluses—from unfunded shocks resolved through price-level adjustment. The identification delivers impulse responses and historical decompositions showing that funded deficit shocks are approximately Ricardian and have limited real effects, while unfunded deficit shocks raise inflation and real GDP growth, lower nominal returns on government debt on impact, and account for a meaningful share of post-1960 inflation fluctuations.
